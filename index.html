04 / The wrong diagnosis

The standard story is a workforce story. The data tells a different one.

Vermont has a primary care shortage. Train more doctors. Recruit harder. Throw scholarships at the problem.

Vermont has more primary care clinicians per capita than any other state.

The shortage in access is real, but it isn't because we don't have enough doctors. It's because of how we pay them.

05 / Where the money goes

Vermont is the only state in this group without a binding primary care spending target.

Most of the U.S. healthcare dollar flows to specialty care, hospitals, and pharmaceuticals. Primary care, the part of the system most strongly linked to better health outcomes, gets the smallest cut. Other states have responded by writing primary care investment into law as a measurable obligation. Vermont has not, at least not yet.

Definitions of primary care spending vary by state, so direct percentage comparisons are imprecise. The clearer comparison is structural: three states have made primary care investment a measurable, enforceable obligation. Vermont has not. The 2026 Milbank Primary Care Scorecard reports Vermont's commercial spending share at 6.5% under a narrow definition, the highest measured share in the country, but Vermont currently has no binding target tying that figure to access outcomes.

06 / How each mechanism works

A target on paper is not the same as a target with teeth.

The three states use different regulatory tools to make their primary care investment goals enforceable. Vermont's pending bills can borrow from any of them. The choice between them is less about ambition and more about which agency holds the lever.

Rhode Island

The Office of the Health Insurance Commissioner reviews insurer rate filings every year. The 10.7% standard sits inside that rate review process, so when an insurer files rates that don't meet the standard, OHIC can reject them.

The mechanism is enforcement through existing rate review.

Oregon

SB 934 set the 12% target in statute and required Coordinated Care Organizations to report annually on progress. The reporting requirement created public accountability without requiring direct enforcement.

The mechanism is statutory target plus mandatory reporting.

Colorado

The Division of Insurance issued Regulation 4-2-96 under existing authority, requiring the primary care share to increase by one percentage point per year. The annual increment removed the need for a fixed target debate.

The mechanism is incremental growth through regulatory rulemaking.

08 / The Vermont path

The bills are already drafted.

Vermont's 2025–26 legislative session already has multiple primary care payment reform proposals on the table. The legal scaffolding mostly exists. Whether any of it passes is a political question, not a technical one.

  • S.197 Establishes a Primary Care Payment Reform Program, operational by July 1, 2027.
  • H.680 Creates a primary care access reform program with monthly capitated payments and no patient cost-sharing for routine primary care.
  • Pending target 15% of total Vermont health spending directed toward primary care.
  • Missing The political will to pass them.
Ellen Oxfeld and Vermont's universal primary care movement argue for going much further than payment reform: funding primary care like a public good, outside the insurance system entirely. A spending floor isn't that, but it's a step in the same direction.
